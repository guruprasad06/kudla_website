History Behind The name Mangaluru

The name of the city, now officially Mangaluru, is primarily derived from the Hindu deity Mangaladevi, the presiding goddess of the historic Mangaladevi Temple located in the city. According to local legend: A princess named Premaladevi from Malabar became a disciple of the Nath sect founder, Matsyendranath, and was renamed Mangaladevi. She fell ill and passed away near the present-day temple site. The Mangaladevi Temple was consecrated in her honour, and the city was subsequently named after the temple. The original name in Kannada/Tulu, Mangalapura (meaning "auspicious city," with mangala meaning auspicious and pura meaning town/city) or Mangaluru (uru meaning town/city), was later anglicised to 'Mangalore' during the British rule. The city's official name was restored to Mangaluru by the Karnataka government in 2014. It is also known as Kudla in Tulu (meaning "junction" due to the confluence of the Netravati and Gurupura rivers) and Kodial in Konkani.

Daiva Aradhana :

Daiva Aradhane (Deity/Spirit Worship), also known as Bhuta Kola, is an ancient ritualistic practice of appeasing local guardian spirits (Bhutas or Daivas) unique to the Tulu-speaking region. The night-long ceremony involves a ritual specialist, known as the Patri or impersonator, who, after extensive preparation with dramatic makeup, heavy costumes, and ceremonial ornaments, enters a trance-like state believed to be a temporary possession by the spirit. During this possession, the Daiva delivers justice, provides prophecies, and offers blessings to the community, addressing their grievances and ensuring the land's prosperity. This profound socio-religious ritual is an integral part of the Tuluva social and spiritual governance, maintaining order and connecting the people with their ancestral protectors.

Kambula :

Kambala is an annual, traditional buffalo race held in the slushy, water-filled paddy fields of coastal Karnataka, typically taking place between November and March. Originating as a festive event to mark the end of the harvesting season and to honor local deities for a good crop, the sport involves pairs of specially trained racing buffaloes being guided by a farmer (the runner) through a muddy track. The competition is a thrilling spectacle that showcases the deep bond between the farming community and their livestock, celebrated with massive crowds who gather to cheer on the racers. Modern Kambala has evolved into an organized sporting event with different categories, though it remains a powerful symbol of the region's agrarian heritage.

Pili Nalike :

Pili Nalike, or the Tiger Dance, is an energetic and ritualistic folk dance unique to Mangalore and the Tulu Nadu region, prominently performed during the Navaratri and Dasara festivities. Troupes of young men paint their bare bodies meticulously to resemble tigers, leopards, or cheetahs, a process that takes several hours. The performers then dance vigorously in the streets and in front of temples to the pulsating beats of the Thaase (percussion drums), exhibiting tremendous stamina and acrobatics, including fire breathing, handstands, and bending backward to pick up objects with their mouths. This dance is performed to honor Goddess Durga, whose favored animal is the tiger, and is a major highlight of the region's celebratory street processions.

Yakshagana :

Yakshagana is a traditional, elaborate folk theatre form that is central to the culture of coastal Karnataka. Literally meaning "the song of the Yaksha (celestial beings)," this all-night performance art brilliantly combines dance, music, improvised dialogue, costume, and dramatic staging to narrate stories primarily drawn from Hindu epics like the Ramayana and the Mahabharata. The performers wear spectacular, vibrant costumes, intricate face paint, and towering headgear, embodying characters ranging from noble kings to powerful demons. The performance is guided by the Bhagavata (lead singer/narrator), who directs the play with singing and narration, accompanied by traditional percussion instruments like the Maddale and the Chande, creating a unique, electrifying theatrical experience.
